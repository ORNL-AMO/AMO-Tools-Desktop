Gas temperature

Measure flue gas temperature by using "proper" temperature probe. Type of temperature probe depends on flue gas temperature and composition at the point of measurement. In most cases a commercially available thermocouple probe is used.

Units: oF or oC

Excess Air

The excess air value represents excess air used in burners plus all air entering the furnace and going as flue gases form the furnace. The value is expressed in terms of percentage of stoichiometric air required for the fuel used.

Units: %
Minimum Value Allowed: 0

O2 in Flue Gas

The volumetric percentage of oxygen present in the flue gas. Flue gas O2 (dry basis) reading is taken at the same location where flue gas temperature is measured to maintain accuracy of the calculations.

Units: %
Minimum Value Allowed: 0
Maximum Value Allowed: 21

Combustion air temperature

Combustion air temperature (Celsius) entering the burners. Use temperature recorder/indicator reading or actual asurement using a temperature gage, thermocouple or estimate based on the furnace supplier's specification.

Units: oF or oC

Fuel temperature

Temperature of the incoming fuel. In most cases fuel is supplied at near ambient temperature however in some cases it can be at a higher temperature when it is preheated.

Units: oF or oC

Moisture in Air

The moisture of the air (mass water / mass dry air). Can be determined with a psychometric chart.

Units: %
Minimum Value Allowed: 0

Ash Discharge Temperature

Temperature of the ash in the flue gas outlet.

Units: oF or oC

Unburned Carbon in Ash

Percent of unburned carbon in Ash.

Units: %
Minimum Value Allowed: 0
Maximum Value Allowed: 100

Savings Suggestions
  • Maintain appropriate level of oxygen in flue gases by controlling air-fuel ratio for the burners
  • Maintain and control burner operations to eliminate formation of soot or combustible gases such as carbon monoxide and hydrogen in flue gases
  • Eliminate or reduce air leakage in the furnace. See "Opening Losses" section
  • Consider use of heat recovery from flue gases. Consider use of various methods of heat recovery to reduce flue gas temperature leaving the heating system
  • Use preheated combustion air through use of recuperators or regenerators
  • Where appropriate, consider use of oxygen enrichment of combustion air to reduce mass of flue gases
  • Review process temperature settings and lower it where possible during part-load operations. This can reduce flue gas temperature leaving the heating system
  • Note: These energy saving measures are for guidance only. Not all measures are applicable under all operating conditions. There may additional measures when considering specific situations and the user is encouraged to review and apply the appropriate measures
