Flue Gas Losses Help
Enter measured data to calculate your system's annual savings potential.
  • Maintain appropriate level of oxygen in flue gases by controlling air-fuel ratio for the burners
  • Maintain and control burner operations to eliminate formation of soot or combustible gases such as carbon monoxide and hydrogen in flue gases
  • Eliminate or reduce air leakage in the furnace. See "Opening Losses" section
  • Consider use of heat recovery from flue gases. Consider use of various methods of heat recovery to reduce flue gas temperature leaving the heating system
  • Use preheated combustion air through use of recuperators or regenerators
  • Where appropriate, consider use of oxygen enrichment of combustion air to reduce mass of flue gases
  • Review process temperature settings and lower it where possible during part-load operations. This can reduce flue gas temperature leaving the heating system
Note: These energy saving measures are for guidance only. Not all measures are applicable under all operating conditions. There may additional measures when considering specific situations and the user is encouraged to review and apply the appropriate measures

Flue Gas Temperature
The temperature of the gas exiting the system. This value must be greater than the combustion air temperature. If your flue gas temperature is less than 212℉ then vapor may be condensing in the flue and the calculated available heat may be incorrect.
Measure flue gas temperature by using "proper" temperature probe. Type of temperature probe depends on flue gas temperature and composition at the point of measurement. In most cases a commercially available thermocouple probe is used. Placement of the probe depends on the baseline.

O2 in Flue Gas / Excess Air Dropdown
Choose which value is known for the calculation of Available heat. Using Excess Air is recommended (for higher accuracy) if known.
O2 in Flue Gas / Excess Air Dropdown options include Excess Air or Oxygen in Flue Gas
Excess Air
The excess air value represents excess air used in burners plus all air entering the furnace and going as flue gases form the furnace. The value is expressed in terms of percentage of stoichiometric air required for the fuel used and can be calculated as the difference between the measured O2 and stoichiometric O2 divided by the stoichiometric O2.
This should be minimized while still ensuring adequate air for complete combustion. The absolute maximum value depends on the fuel, with some natural gas mixes near 500% and coal closer to 100%.
O 2 in Flue Gas
Molar (or volumetric) fraction (% Dry) of O2 in outgoing flue gas (ignoring moisture content). Higher O2 in the exhaust indicates higher excess air and a greater amount of heat contained within the flue gas (lowering the available heat).
Flue gas O2 (dry basis) reading is taken at the same location where flue gas temperature is measured to maintain accuracy of the calculations. For many applications this is less than 10%, though a few systems (e.g., thermal oxidizers, paint shop ovens) it may be higher, but still below 21%.

Combustion Air Temperature
Combustion air temperature (Celsius) entering the burners. Use temperature recorder/indicator reading or actual asurement using a temperature gage, thermocouple or estimate based on the furnace supplier's specification.
This value must be less than the flue gas temperature.
Fuel Temperature
Temperature of the incoming fuel. For solid fuels, this can often be estimated to be ambient temperature. For gases, this can often be estimated to be slightly less than ambient temperature (about 10℉) due to the Joule-Thompson Effect. It will be higher if preheating is used.
When preheating fuels, the maximum temperature depends on various various factors, including the specific fuel type, industry standards, safety considerations, and regulatory requirements. For natural gas, typically it is not recommended and the maximum temperature is 300℉. For heavy fuels (fuel oil and residual oils) the max is near 400℉. Other gaseous fuels, preheating is not recommended, with a maximum temperature of 150℉.

Moisture in Air
The moisture of the air (mass water / mass dry air). Can be determined with a psychrometric chart.
Generally, a moisture content of up to 5% is considered acceptable for combustion air in typical industrial applications.
