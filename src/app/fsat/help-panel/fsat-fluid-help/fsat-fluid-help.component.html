Method to Establish Gas Density

Method to Establish Gas Density has 4 options for the determination of gas density: using relative humidity, wet bulb temperature, dew point temperature or already knowing the gas density. Choose which option is appropriate for what is known.

Dry Bulb Temperature

The true thermodynamic temperature, measured shielded from radiation and moisture. This indicates the amount of heat in the air without including the amount of moisture. Taken with a standard thermometer.

Static Pressure

The portion of the air pressure due to compression of the gas, at the inlet of the fan.

Wet Bulb Temperature

The lowest temperature that is achieved by using evaporative cooling on a water-soaked surface. Read using a thermometer covered in a water-soaked cloth over which air is passed, such as a sling psychrometer. Specific Gravity

Ratio of the density of the gas at the inlet of the Fan to the density of air at normal/standard temperature and pressure (68 ℉, 1 atm). The Specific Gravity for Air is 1. Specific Gravity for flue gases ranges between approximately 0.2 and 1.1, depending on temperature and specific composition.

Specific Heat Gas (Cp)

The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of the gas one degree for each unit of mass. For air this is 0.24 Btu/lb ℉, for flue gases, this ranges between 0.248 and 0.320 Btu/lb ℉, depending on temperature and composition.

Relative Humidity

The relative humidity is the ratio of the partial pressure of water vapor in the air to the equilibrium vapor pressure of water. Can be measured with a hygrometer or found via a weather report.

Dew Point

The temperature that air is cooled enough to become saturated with water vapor. Can be found via weather reports.

Gas Density

The total density of the gas at the inlet of the Fan.
