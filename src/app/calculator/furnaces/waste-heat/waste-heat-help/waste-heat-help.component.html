Waste Heat For Absorption Chillers
This tool is used to calculate energy savings and economics when exhaust gas (waste) heat is used to provide energy for an absorption chiller. The absorption chiller system can be used to replace an existing mechanical compressor based chiller system that uses electricity. In the waste heat driven system a heat exchanger is used to heat water using heat of exhaust gases from heating equipment such as a furnace, oven, heater, kiln, boiler etc. The calculator requires data on exhaust gases (standard flue gas analysis or FGA) as well as water flow and inlet and outlet conditions as required by the chiller supplier. Use of absorption chiller requires cooling water supply for the condenser part of the system. In most cases it is necessary to have surplus cooling tower capacity or installation of a water cooling system such as a cooling tower or air to water heat exchanger (dry cooler). Overall economics must include cost of the absorption chiller system, its installation and cost of installing and connecting a water cooling system. The calculator gives savings in electricity, its cost and equivalent fuel energy savings as well as reduction in CO2 emission corresponding to electrical energy saving.
Available Heat
The gross quantity of heat released within a combustion chamber minus both the dry flue gas loss and the moisture loss. It represents the quantity of heat remaining for useful purposes (and to balance losses to walls, openings, and conveyors).

More simply, available heat is the fraction of the heat available to be used over the fuel heat input into the system.

Available heat =
Heat input - Heat in flue gases Heat input


Available heat can be estimated via charts (https://www.energy.gov/sites/prod/files/2014/05/f16/check_burner_air2.pdf) or calculated. MEASUR can help estimate the available heat if several parameters are known (such as, excess air or oxygen in flue and flue, fuel, and combustion air temperatures).

This is used to estimate the true amount of fuel wasted for a given heat loss. If only wanting to estimate the heat lost directly, set available heat to 100%.
Annual Operating Hours
Number of hours per year the lighting is on. This is not necessarily how many hours the facility is operating. Be sure to include normally scheduled overtime.
Heat Input
The amount of heat required for the heating process, including all losses. Can be found via calculation (using the Process Heating Assessment tool), operating data or gas bills
Proposed Heat Exchanger Effectiveness
Efficiency of the exhaust gas – water heat exchanger. This value can be in the range of 50% to 85%. 70% is an average value if unknown.
Hot Water Temperature In
Temperature of the hot water entering the chiller system. This is usually given by the chiller system supplier or measured at the source, if unknown, use 180 to 200 °F. as starting value.
Hot Water Temperature Out
Temperature of the hot water leaving the chiller system. This is usually given by the chiller system supplier or measured at the source, if unknown, use 160 to 185 °F. as starting value.
Chiller COP
The Coefficient of Performance - COP - is the basic parameter used to report efficiency of refrigerant based systems. COP is the ratio between useful cooling or heating output and power input. This is usually given by the chiller system supplier. Value of 0.7 can be considered as a good starting point for this type of analysis.
Chiller Efficiency
Overall efficiency of the chiller system, accounting for losses in all the sub-systems.
Compressor COP
The Coefficient of Performance (COP) of the original system’s compressor. The COP value depends on a lot of factors but can be obtained from the system supplier. Value of 5.0 can be considered as a good starting point for this type of analysis.
Electricity Cost
Cost of electricity per kWh.
