Air Heating Using Flue Gas Help
This calculator can be used to estimate maximum air flow that can be heated by using exhaust gas heat of flue gases from a furnace, oven, or boiler. The hot air can be used as a makeup air for buildings during cold weather months or it can be used as heated makeup air for ovens or other processes. It is necessary to know cold air entering the heat recovery unit and the desired hot air temperature; leaving the heat recovery unit or a heat exchanger. It is also necessary to know the details of operation of the oven or furnace from where the hot exhaust gases are used.

Moisture in Air
The moisture of the air (mass water / mass dry air). Can be determined with a psychrometric chart.
Generally, a moisture content of up to 5% is considered acceptable for combustion air in typical industrial applications.

Furnace Flue Gas Temperature
The temperature of the gas exiting the system. This value must be greater than the combustion air temperature. If your flue gas temperature is less than 212℉ then vapor may be condensing in the flue and the calculated available heat may be incorrect.
Measure flue gas temperature by using "proper" temperature probe. Type of temperature probe depends on flue gas temperature and composition at the point of measurement. In most cases a commercially available thermocouple probe is used. Placement of the probe depends on the baseline.

Excess Air
The excess air value represents excess air used in burners plus all air entering the furnace and going as flue gases form the furnace. The value is expressed in terms of percentage of stoichiometric air required for the fuel used and can be calculated as the difference between the measured O2 and stoichiometric O2 divided by the stoichiometric O2.
This should be minimized while still ensuring adequate air for complete combustion. The absolute maximum value depends on the fuel, with some natural gas mixes near 500% and coal closer to 100%.
O2 in Flue Gas
Molar (or volumetric) fraction (% Dry) of O2 in outgoing flue gas (ignoring moisture content). Higher O2 in the exhaust indicates higher excess air and a greater amount of heat contained within the flue gas (lowering the available heat).
Flue gas O2 (dry basis) reading is taken at the same location where flue gas temperature is measured to maintain accuracy of the calculations. For many applications this is less than 10%, though a few systems (e.g., thermal oxidizers, paint shop ovens) it may be higher, but still below 21%.

Combustion Air Temperature
Combustion air temperature (Celsius) entering the burners. Use temperature recorder/indicator reading or actual asurement using a temperature gage, thermocouple or estimate based on the furnace supplier's specification.
This value must be less than the flue gas temperature.
Average Firing Rate
The average firing rate of the furnace, oven, boiler, etc. This can be obtained from measurement, the gas bills (or other meters), or the equipment supplier (design specifications). It can also be estimated using the Process Heating Assessment.
Air Flow
The amount of air currently heated by another heat source (air to be heated by exhaust gas)
Inlet Air Temperature
Average temperature of the air heated by heat source or exhaust gas. Likely to be ambient or external air temperature. This can be a measured value or from the equipment supplier.
Outlet Air Temperature
Average temperature of air after being heated. For HVAC systems, in most cases this will be less than 250 deg. F. There may be exceptions to this when air is used for other than HVAC. This can be a measured value or from the equipment supplier.
Current Heater Efficiency
The efficiency of the heat source currently used to heat air. Heater efficiency depends on the heater design and size. In most cases it can be assumed to be in a range of 75% to 95%.
Proposed Heat Exchanger Effectiveness
The efficiency of the heat source currently used to heat air. Heater efficiency depends on the heater design and size. In most cases it can be assumed to be in a range of 50% to 70%.
