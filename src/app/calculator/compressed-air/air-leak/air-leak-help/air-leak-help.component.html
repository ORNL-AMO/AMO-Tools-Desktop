Compressed Air - Leak Survey Help
This calculator is used to quantify the energy savings associated with reducing compressed air leaks.
This calculator can be used to quantify the electricity saved from reducing compressed air leaks. Compressed air leaks are a major source of wasted energy sometimes accounting for 20-30% of the compressor’s output. Compressed air leaks can contribute to the following problems:
Fluctuations in system pressure
Excess compressor capacity
Decreased service life and increased maintenance due to unnecessary cycling and increased run time.

Method of Measurement
Choose the method to be used to determine compressed air consumption.
Estimate - The most high-level way to determine the leak rate is to estimate it based on the visual and audible clues.
Decibel Method - The decibels method is used when an Ultrasonic probe is used to scan for leaks. Use the dB vs CFM chart that comes along with the specific leak detector to estimate loss.
Bag Method – The bag method estimates air loss by counting the time it takes to fill a trash bag.
Orifice Method - Estimation of air loss using the pressure at the point of use and the diameter of the orifice. The user must know the air pressure at the point of use for this to be accurate.

Compressor Control Type Adjustment Factor
Select your compressor control type from the dropdown list. Different compressor control strategies react differently to reduced air demand and it is important to take this into consideration when estimating the energy reduction by reducing the demand. This value is applied to the electricity savings, not to the electricity consumption itself.
Values for associated fraction of average power are based off the NREL Compressed Air Evaluation Protocol
IBV - Inlet Butterfly Value
IGV - Inlet Guide Vanes
Each CCAF value represents the slope of the performance curve when operating within the 40% to 80% capacity range as this is a common operating range for a trim compressor.

Compressor Type
Select a compressor type from the dropdown list. To find the savings in terms of energy (kWh) we need to know the electricity consumed by the compressor to produce unit compressed air (specific power of the compressor). These values can be found from previous compressed air studies or from the CAGI data sheets for the specific compressor found online. In a multi-compressor system the energy savings associated with reduced air demand are usually only seen at the trim compressor, hence the “Specific Power” and the “Control-Type Adjustment Factor” are to be specified by the trim compressor.
Note: It is recommended that the Specific Power of the compressor is defined by the user for most accurate results.
Note: These values are only valid for compressors operating at full load. Please check data sheets for more appropriate numbers if not at full load
