Establish Gas Density Help
This section establishes a gas density as a reference to establish the gas density within the fan. The location for which base gas density data is inputted could be one of the Planar Locations 1 through 5, or for an alternate location outside of Planes Nos. 1 through 5 such as in the plant general spaces or the outdoors (i.e. if the fan draws ambient air from plant spaces or the outdoors).
Gas Type
Select either ‘Air’ or ‘Other Gases’ depending on the type of gas.
Method to Establish Gas Density
There are four options for establishing gas density:
Method Description
Relative Humidity % Calculate base gas density from dry bulb temperature, static pressure, barometric pressure, gas specific gravity, and gas humidity data.
Wet Bulb Temperature Calculate base gas density from dry bulb temperature, static pressure, barometric pressure, gas specific gravity, wet bulb temperature and specific heat of the gas.
Gas Dew Point Calculate base gas density from dry bulb temperature, static pressure, barometric pressure, gas specific gravity, and gas dew point.
Use Custom Density If gas density is already known, it can be manually inputted.
Dry Bulb Temp
Reference dry bulb temperature calculated as the temperature of a dry surface in a vapor-gas environment.
Static Pressure
Reference static pressure (relative to barometric pressure).
Barometric Pressure
Reference barometric pressure.
Gas Specific Gravity
The ratio of molecular weight of the gas in question to that of air. For air, this is 1.0. For flue gases around 700 °C, this is about 0.3.
Relative Humidity
Percent relative humidity, a measure of the amount of water vapor present in the gas. This is usually provided in weather reports.
Wet Bulb Temp
The adiabatic saturation temperature sometimes calculated as the temperature read by a thermometer covered in water-soaked cloth over which the gas is passed.
Specific Heat Gas
The amount of heat required to raise the temperature of the gas one degree for each unit of mass. For air, this is 0.24 Btu/lb °F; for flue gases, this ranges between 0.248 and 0.320 Btu/lb °F, depending on temperature and composition.
Gas Dew Point
The temperature where air is cooled enough to become saturated with water vapor. For atmospheric air, this can be found via weather reports. Otherwise, it’s a result of the gases composition and pressure.
Gas Density
If gas density is already known, it can be entered here.
