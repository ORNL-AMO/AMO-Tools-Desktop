Adjust operating conditions to compare energy use and energy costs of different motor drive types.
Approximately one-third of the electric motors in the industrial and commercial sectors use belt drives most of which are V-belts, which can have a peak efficiency of 95% or more at the time of installation. Efficiency is dependent on pulley size, pulley wear, V-belt alignment, transmitted torque, and under or oversizing belts for load requirements. Efficiency can deteriorate by as much as 5% over time if slippage occurs because the belt is not periodically retensioned, which requires downtime.
A notched belt has grooves or notches that run perpendicular to the beltâ€™s length, which reduces the bending resistance of the belt. They run cooler, last longer, and are about 2% more efficient than standard V-belts.
Synchronous belts are toothed and require the installation of mating grooved sprockets. These belts operate with a consistent efficiency of 98% and maintain their efficiency over a wide load range. On critical drives, a synchronous belt, which requires no retensioning, not only improves energy efficiency but also eliminates downtime.

Average Motor Load
Also called load factor, this is the ratio between actual load and rated load. Often motors are installed larger (more horsepower) than required by the driven load to compensate for time degradation and variations in the demand. The Percent Load is best determined by measurement of the current and voltage, percent loaded can then be calculated (the Percent Load Estimation Calculator can help).

If a measurement is not possible, then it can be estimated using the following rule-of-thumb. If the motor is part of OEM equipment then 100% is probably correct, if the motor was installed as part of a system and loading is not known 75% or 80% Is a conservative estimate.

Maximum efficiency is usually near 75% of rated load. Overloaded motors can overheat and lose efficiency. Underloaded motors, those loaded below 50% of rated load, are inefficient and exhibit low power factor.
